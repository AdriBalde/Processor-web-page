What is ALU?

An arithmetic logic unit (ALU) is a digital circuit used to perform arithmetic and logical operations. Examples of arithmetic operations are addition, subtraction, multiplication and division; and some examples of operations are value comparisons such as NOT, AND and OR. In addition to the logical and arithmetic operators, the ALU has a number of registers for storing data, and bits of information about the results, also called flags.

Where to find it

This unit represents the fundamental building block of a computer's central processing unit (CPU). Modern CPUs contain very powerful and complex ALUs. In addition to the ALU, modern CPUs also contain a control unit (CU).

Apart from the CPU, an ALU can be found in all kinds of electronic circuits and devices. For example, in a digital wristwatch that allows the constant addition of one second. Other examples are found in graphics, sound or video cards, high-definition TV sets, and CD players.

Components

It is in these small and complex devices that we find the following components:

  • Addition Devices: They are in charge of performing the aforementioned arithmetic operations.
  • Registers: These contain the Operands that allow the operations to be carried out, and are provided by the Control Unit.
  • Partial Results: Fragments of calculations that were performed.
  • Final Results: Actual results of the calculations performed.
  • Calculation Control: Device that is in charge of controlling, redirecting and correcting the errors that may have been made in the operations performed.

Functioning

We have previously mentioned the Control Unit, and we must define it as a device that is in charge of sending to the ALU all the orders and operations it must perform, as well as transporting the Final Results it has obtained to other components.

In this way, we can define how it works as follows: The control unit issues the actions to be performed. Then the arithmetic logic unit processes the data it has received and finally the control unit analyses the results it has obtained and sends them to other devices.

Explanation of logical operations

To perform arithmetic and logical operations, two integers in the form of bits are applied to the input of the ALU and another terminal receives the instructions to manipulate that input. These instructions, as the unit name implies, are mathematical calculations such as addition. Once the ALU has performed the commanded operations, it outputs the value of the result.
